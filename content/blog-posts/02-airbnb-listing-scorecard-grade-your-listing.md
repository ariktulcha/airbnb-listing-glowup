## Angle

A host does not need a perfect listing. They need to identify the weakest parts of the listing presentation and fix the highest-impact issues first.

## Draft introduction

Most Airbnb hosts know when their listing could look better, but they do not always know what to fix first. Is it the photos? The title? The description? The amenities? The price?

A simple scorecard helps separate guesswork from practical next steps. In 15 minutes, you can grade the listing the way a guest experiences it: first impression, photo tour, clarity, trust, and reason to choose.

## Main sections

### 1. Score the cover photo

Ask whether the cover photo is bright, specific, and competitive. It should show the strongest reason to click, not just a random room.

### 2. Score the first five photos

The first five images should tell the core story of the stay. If they feel repetitive or confusing, guests may move on before reading.

### 3. Score the full photo set

Look for missing rooms, weak lighting, duplicates, awkward crops, and photos that create questions instead of answers.

### 4. Score the title

A strong Airbnb title is specific. It gives guests a reason to care: view, location, design, group size, work setup, outdoor space, or unique feature.

### 5. Score the description

The description should help guests imagine the stay, not just list features. Lead with the experience and clarify the practical details.

### 6. Score trust and clarity

Guests want fewer surprises. Make parking, bed sizes, stairs, noise, workspace, check-in, and limitations clear.

### 7. Score competitive positioning

Open three nearby listings. If they look more polished or easier to understand, your listing may need a presentation refresh.

## Score interpretation

- **85–100:** Strong listing. Make small refinements and test cover/title options.
- **70–84:** Good property, likely presentation gaps. Fix the weakest category first.
- **55–69:** Under-presented. A structured listing refresh is likely worthwhile.
- **Below 55:** Guests may not be seeing the real value of the property. Prioritize photos, title, and first-screen clarity.
